CFA Level 1
Topic: Fixed Income
A bond with a 6% annual coupon, 10-year maturity, and a face value of $1,000 is currently priced at $1,074.39, implying a yield-to-maturity of 5%. If the bond's yield instantaneously rises to 6%, which of the following best explains why the price change will be smaller in magnitude than if the yield had instead fallen by 1% to 4%?
  • A. Positive convexity causes price increases from yield declines to exceed price decreases from equivalent yield increases.
  • B. The bond's modified duration increases as yields rise, amplifying losses on the downside.
  • C. Negative convexity causes the price-yield relationship to become linear at higher yield levels.
  • D. Coupon reinvestment risk offsets capital gains when yields fall, making the net price change symmetric.
Reveal Answer & Explanation
Correct: A
For an option-free bond, the price-yield relationship is convex, meaning the curve bows toward the origin. This positive convexity implies that for equal-sized yield changes, the price gain from a yield decrease is always larger than the price loss from a yield increase. Duration provides a linear approximation of price sensitivity, but convexity captures the curvature that makes the relationship asymmetric. Reinvestment risk and changes in modified duration are secondary effects and do not directly explain this asymmetry.

Basis Risk: The Hidden Hedge Imperfection
Why hedges almost never work perfectly, and what that means for risk managers.

Basis risk arises when the price of a hedging instrument does not move in perfect lockstep with the price of the asset being hedged. In theory, a perfect hedge eliminates all price risk by taking an equal and opposite position in a correlated instrument. In practice, the 'basis' — defined as the spot price of the asset minus the futures price of the hedging contract — fluctuates over time, leaving residual risk even in carefully constructed hedges. This imperfection is not a flaw in the hedger's execution; it is an inherent feature of using standardized derivatives to hedge non-standardized exposures.

Basis risk became painfully visible during the 2008 financial crisis, when correlations between mortgage-backed securities and the credit default swaps used to hedge them broke down sharply. Institutions that believed they had hedged their mortgage exposure discovered that their hedges tracked poorly as liquidity dried up and the basis widened dramatically. A more routine example occurs in commodity markets: an airline hedging jet fuel costs using crude oil futures accepts basis risk because jet fuel and crude oil prices, while correlated, do not move identically. Seasonal refinery dynamics, regional supply disruptions, and crack spread volatility all contribute to a basis that can widen or narrow unpredictably.

For investors and risk managers, understanding basis risk is critical when evaluating the true effectiveness of any hedging strategy. The hedge ratio — the proportion of an exposure that is hedged — should be calculated using the historical correlation and relative volatility between the hedged item and the hedging instrument, not assumed to be 1:1. Managers should also monitor the basis continuously, as a deteriorating basis can turn a well-intentioned hedge into a source of additional losses. In accounting terms, under IFRS 9 and ASC 815, ineffective portions of hedges must flow through earnings, making basis risk not just an economic concern but a financial reporting one as well.

Key Takeaways

Basis risk is the residual risk remaining after a hedge is placed, caused by imperfect correlation between the hedging instrument and the underlying exposure.
Basis can widen dramatically during market stress, as seen in 2008, turning hedges into sources of additional loss rather than protection.
Effective hedge ratios should be calibrated using historical volatility and correlation data, and monitored continuously rather than set and forgotten.

Japanese Lesson
Beginner — JLPT N5 / N4
貯金
chokin
savings; money saved up
noun
毎月、給料の一部を貯金しています。
Maitsuki, kyuuryou no ichibu wo chokin shite imasu.
Every month, I save part of my salary.
Grammar: 〜しています
The pattern 〜しています (verb stem + shite imasu) expresses an ongoing action or habit. Here it means 'I am (habitually) saving,' describing something the speaker does regularly rather than a one-time event.
Think of 'chokin' as 'cho-ching!' — the satisfying sound of coins dropping into a piggy bank every time you save money.
